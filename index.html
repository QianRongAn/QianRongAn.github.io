Master's Thesis: Bioinformatics Analysis of COVID-19-Associated Immune Repertoire and Specific TCRs

Chapter 1: Introduction

1.1 Immunomics Background

Immune System: The immune system is divided into two components: innate immunity and adaptive immunity. Adaptive immunity primarily involves T cells (TCR), B cells, and MHC molecules.

Innate and adaptive branches of the immune system

1.2 Immune Repertoire and Tumor Neoantigens

The immune repertoire exhibits high diversity and is crucial for studying adaptive immunity. Research also focuses on tumor neoantigens.

Mechanism of tumor neoantigen formation:

  • Originates from somatic mutations.
  • Mutant peptides are processed into 8-11 mer peptides.
  • Subsequently captured by MHC class I molecules.
  • Finally expressed on the surface of tumor cells, forming tumor neoantigens, thereby activating tumor-specific T cells.

1.3 TCR Research in the Context of COVID-19

In the context of the global COVID-19 pandemic, research on SARS-CoV-2-associated virus-specific TCRs and diverse immune repertoires is crucial. Key findings:

  • T cells can clear SARS virus in the absence of antibodies or when the innate immune system is not activated.
  • SARS-CoV-2-specific T cells persist, forming long-lasting memory responses.
  • T cell responses can activate humoral responses and may precede antibodies in asymptomatic or mild disease.

Core Research Content and Directions

This research includes bioinformatics analysis of TCR repertoires from COVID-19 patients and tumor neoantigen prediction.

Main research directions:

  • TCR repertoire characterization: Investigation of CDR3 clonotype features, diversity, V/J gene preferences, overlap, etc.
  • Classifier development and improvement: Construction of a COVID-19 symptom classifier, algorithm improvement for COVID-19-associated TCRs, interpretation of peptide contributions, and validation with additional datasets.
  • Study populations: Involves immune repertoire sequencing of peripheral blood T cells, covering acute phase, transition phase, recovery phase, healthy individuals, and vaccinated populations.

Chapter 2: Materials and Methods

2.1 Data Sources and Description

  • COVID-19 data: Derived from the ImmuneCODE database, including TCR sequences from over 1,400 subjects and more than 135,000 high-confidence SARS-CoV-2-specific TCRs.
  • Liver cancer data: Single-cell and exome sequencing data were obtained from the GSA-Human system with GSA accession numbers HRA001748 and HRA001749. The project included 124 liver cancer patients (79 with HCC) and 8 liver cancer mice.

2.2 Bioinformatics Analysis Pipeline

Immune repertoire analysis pipeline: Raw input → Alignment → Clone integration → VDJmatch → GLIPH → R language → Machine learning classifier.

Bioinformatics workflow for immune repertoire analysis
Technical description: The immune repertoire bioinformatics analysis pipeline is as follows: Patient sequences are aligned to the TCR reference genome in the International ImMunoGeneTics information system (IMGT) database. After identifying V, D, and J gene segment sequences, they are assembled into CDR3 amino acid sequences. VDJmatch can then be used to annotate TCRs with known antigen-specific epitopes. GLIPH can cluster TCRs with similar antigen specificities. R language is used for comprehensive analysis of the immune repertoire, and finally, machine learning classifiers are used for prediction.
Classifier construction method
Classifier construction method: For each TCR pair, we calculated its frequency across different groups and used Fisher's exact test on a 2×2 contingency table to assess the association between TCRs and phenotypic status, resulting in the COVID-19-associated TCR summary table shown. A P-value threshold was manually set, and sequences below the threshold were considered virus-related and classified as 1, while others were classified as 0 for model training.
Tumor neoantigen prediction pipeline flowchart

Tumor neoantigen prediction pipeline

  • Input data: Whole-exome sequencing (WES) data from tumor and normal tissues.
  • Somatic mutation detection: Alignment to the reference genome using tools such as Mutect2 to identify somatic mutations (e.g., SNVs, Indels).
  • HLA typing: Inference of patient HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) alleles using tools such as OptiType or HLAscan.
  • Candidate neoantigen prediction: Generation of mutant peptides from mutation information and prediction of their binding affinity (IC50 values) to the inferred HLA molecules using tools such as NetMHCpan.
  • Neoantigen filtering and selection: Selection of tumor neoantigens with high immunogenicity based on binding affinity thresholds (e.g., IC50 < 500 nM) and expression levels.

3.2 Machine Learning Results

Based on TCR repertoire data, we constructed and evaluated multiple machine learning classifiers to accurately distinguish SARS-CoV-2 infection status from healthy populations:

  • Algorithm comparison: Compared the performance of multiple machine learning algorithms including RF, SVM, XGBOOST in classification tasks.
  • Performance evaluation: Scatter plots and ROC curves demonstrated the accuracy, sensitivity, and specificity of classifiers in distinguishing different groups (e.g., acute phase vs. healthy group).
Scatter plots and ROC curves of classifiers between groups. (A) Acute phase vs. transition phase; (B) Acute phase vs. recovery phase; (C) Acute phase vs. healthy group.
ROC curves of classifiers between groups. (A) Acute phase vs. healthy group; (B) Transition phase vs. healthy group; (C) Recovery phase vs. healthy group; (D) Acute phase vs. transition phase; (E) Transition phase vs. recovery phase; (F) Acute phase vs. recovery phase.
ROC curve interpretation: The area under the ROC curve (AUC) represents classification performance, with values closer to 1 indicating better classification. As shown in the figure, the SVM-rbf algorithm performed optimally across multiple comparison groups, achieving an AUC of 0.931 in distinguishing acute phase from healthy groups.

From the scatter plots, it can be observed that when acute phase and transition phase patients are grouped together, their immune repertoire sequences are difficult to distinguish, resulting in suboptimal classification performance in machine learning with smaller AUC values. This may be due to the similarity of their immune repertoires. In contrast, the immune repertoires of acute phase and recovery phase patients differ significantly from healthy individuals, resulting in better classifier performance that can distinguish approximately 90% of the population.

The classifier was further applied to distinguish between post-vaccination populations and COVID-19 patients:

ROC curves of classifiers in post-vaccination populations vs. COVID-19 transition and recovery phase populations. (A) D0 group vs. transition group; (B) PB28 group vs. transition group; (C) D0 group vs. recovery group; (D) PB28 group vs. recovery group.

Figures A-F display the classification performance of six classification scenarios among four population groups. Subsequently, we added two post-vaccination groups for comparison with transition and recovery phase patients. Here, D0 represents the group at day 1 post-vaccination, and PB28 represents the group at day 28 post-vaccination. We found that populations 28 days post-vaccination are most similar to the recovery phase.

Classification performance of classifiers in post-vaccination populations vs. healthy populations. (A) D0 group vs. healthy group; (B) PB28 group vs. healthy group.

Classification between vaccinated groups and healthy individuals showed moderate performance, with 82% of the D0 group distinguishable from healthy individuals, while the PB28 group showed lower performance at 76%. A possible reason is that post-vaccination populations are more similar to healthy individuals compared to COVID-19-infected patients, and 28 days post-vaccination, after immune system renewal, the immune status is more "healthy" compared to initial pathogen exposure.

ROC curves and scatter plots of classifiers in vaccinated populations, healthy individuals, and COVID-19 patients. (A) COVID-19 patient group vs. vaccine group; (B) COVID-19 patient group vs. healthy group; (C) Vaccine group vs. healthy group.

To further validate the above conclusions, the two vaccinated groups were combined and classified separately against COVID-19 patients and healthy individuals. It can be seen that COVID-19 patients share significantly more specific TCRs with both vaccinated and healthy individuals than vaccinated and healthy individuals share with each other. Furthermore, the classification performance distinguishing COVID-19 patients from the other groups is better than that distinguishing vaccinated individuals from healthy individuals. These conclusions further confirm that post-vaccination populations have immune repertoires more similar to healthy individuals compared to COVID-19 patients.

Chapter 4: Summary and Discussion

This research focused on two main directions: COVID-19-associated immune repertoire analysis and tumor neoantigen prediction, successfully achieving the following objectives:

  • Revealing TCR repertoire characteristics: Conducted in-depth bioinformatics analysis of TCR repertoires from COVID-19 patients at different disease stages, revealing differences in TCRβ CDR3 clonotypes, diversity, and V/J gene preferences across different populations.
  • Constructing efficient classifiers: Successfully developed a machine learning classifier based on TCR sequences (using SVM-rbf algorithm) that can accurately distinguish COVID-19 patients from healthy populations, achieving excellent performance metrics in the validation set (AUC as high as 0.931313).
  • Predicting tumor neoantigens: Analyzed WES data from liver cancer patients, predicting potential tumor neoantigens and providing potential targets and research directions for personalized immunotherapy.

Future Work

Future research can be conducted in the following directions:

  • Classifier optimization and clinical translation: Integrate the TCR classifier into user-friendly clinical diagnostic tools, improve model generalization and robustness to achieve early and rapid assessment of SARS-CoV-2 infection status.
  • Neoantigen functional validation: Perform wet lab validation of predicted liver cancer neoantigens (e.g., in vitro T cell stimulation experiments) to confirm their immunogenicity and role in T cell activation.
  • Multi-omics data integration: Further integrate single-cell transcriptome, spatial transcriptome, and TCR repertoire data to deeply analyze the complex regulatory network between the tumor microenvironment and immune cell clonal expansion.
